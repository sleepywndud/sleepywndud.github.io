Perpendicular lines are lines that intersent each other at $90^o$. 

To prove this, you can use the equation below:

$$m_{1}\times m_{2}=-1$$

$m_{1}$ is the gradient of the first line, and $m_{2}$ is the gradient of the second line.

If you don't want to go through that multiplication, **flip your number, and reverse the sign**.

For example, if the gradient of line 1 is $2$, the perpendicular gradient could be found by flipping $2$ into $\frac{1}{2}$, and reversing the sign $\left( -\frac{1}{2} \right)$.

$\therefore$ the $\perp$ gradient would be $-\frac{1}{2}$.

Our school *required* us to write that exact equatio down. Again, other schools may have it differently, so **follow your school/classes way of proving**.

## <u>Perpendicular Bisector</u>

Now, there is the perpendicular bisector.

A perpendicular bisector is a line that bisects another line at exactly $90^o$. To bisect means to divide into two equal parts.

To put it in simple coordinate geometry terms, it is a perpendicular line passing through the midpoint.
