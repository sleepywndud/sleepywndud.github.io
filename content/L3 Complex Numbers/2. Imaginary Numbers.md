# <u>Complex Numbers</u>
Upto now, only 'real' numbers were dealt with in maths.
Real numbers include:
- Rational numbers,
- Irrational numbers,
- Natural numbers,
- etc..

However, in L3 Complex Numbers, we deal with 'complex' numbers -- also known as imaginary numbers. Imaginary numbers cannot be plotted on a standard catesian plane with planes $x$ and $y$.

Imaginary numbers are expressed as $i$.
$i$ is equal to $\sqrt{ -1 }$.

This means that $i^{2}=-1$, and so on.

So, to 'plot' complex numbers such as $i$, we use the Argand plane (or Argand diagram).
An Argand plane consists of 2 axes, similar to the cartesian plane. 
Intead of $x$-axis, it is the *REAL axis*, and instead of the $y$-axis, it is the *IMAGINARY axis*.

In complex numbers, instead of writing the points in coordinate form like:
$$(2,4)$$
we plot points in 'real', and 'imaginary' parts like this:
$$2+4i$$
No brackets, or commas are needed. And that point, we refer to as $z$.
Every complex number $z$ can be written in the form of:
$$z=x+iy$$
.. and can be plotted on the Argand plane.

These four complex numbers are plotted on the argand plane:
$$\left[\space 2+4i, \space 2-4i, \space -2+4i, \space -2-4i \space \right]$$

On quadrant 1, there is $2+4i$, 
on quadrant 2, there is $-2+4i$,
quadrant 3, $-2-4i$, 
and quadrant 4, $2-4i$.
