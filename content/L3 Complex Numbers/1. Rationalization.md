Rationalization is the process of making the denominator into a rational whole number.
For example, when we rationalize:
$$\frac{2}{\sqrt{ 5 }}$$
...we try to remove the $\sqrt{  }$ in the denominator.

To do this, we can multiply the fraction by 1.
However, we don't multiply it by $\frac{1}{1}$, we do $\frac{\sqrt{ 5 }}{\sqrt{ 5 }}$, since $\sqrt{ 5 }\times \sqrt{ 5 }=5$.
Therefore:
$$\frac{2}{\sqrt{ 5 }}\times \frac{\sqrt{ 5 }}{\sqrt{ 5 }}$$
$$\therefore\frac{2\sqrt{ 5 }}{5}$$

# <u>Using Conjugates</u>
(out of context) When we solve a quadratic equation, and the answer is irrational, it will ALWAYS be in the form:
$$\frac{-b\pm \sqrt{ b^{2}-4ac }}{2a}$$
... even if it returns the same number, the sign will be reversed, because of the $\pm$. We call this the 'pair'.

For instance, if $a+\sqrt{ b }$ is one solution, the other solution is $a-\sqrt{ b }$. We call this the *conjugate* -- the opposite sign pairs. 

Similarly, if $a-\sqrt{ b }$ is a surd, then the conjugate is $a+\sqrt{ b }$.

(back to context) When we rationalize, we might have to rationalize the fraction when the denominator is in the form of $a+\sqrt{ b }$. We multiply the denominator by the conjugate. This will result in the form of:
$$(a+\sqrt{ b })(a-\sqrt{ b })$$
This is the 'difference of two squares' pattern, which means that the expanded form of the fraction above will be:
$$a^{2}-b^{2}$$
This removes the $\sqrt{  }$ in the denominator, rationalizing the fraction. 

For example, when we rationalize:
$$\frac{2-\sqrt{ 3 }}{1-\sqrt{ 2 }}$$
We multiply by the conjugate:
$$\frac{2-\sqrt{ 3 }}{1-\sqrt{ 2 }}\times \frac{1+\sqrt{ 2 }}{1+\sqrt{ 2 }}$$
$$\frac{(2-\sqrt{ 3 })(1+\sqrt{ 2 })}{(1-\sqrt{ 2 })(1+\sqrt{ 2 })}$$
$$\frac{2-\sqrt{ 3 }+2\sqrt{ 2 }-\sqrt{ 6 }}{-1}$$
$$\therefore -2+\sqrt{ 3 }-2\sqrt{ 2 }+6$$
