The cubic function. Yes, that's the 'S'-shaped graph. 
A cubic function has an exponent of 3, as you can tell by the name:
$$y=x^3$$
The equation of a cubic is this:
$$y=ax^3+bx^2+cx+d$$
However, we can't translate that effectively, so we have to use:
Respectively the inflection form, and the intercept form.
$$y=a(x-p)^3+q$$
$$y=a(x-\alpha)(x-\beta)(x-\gamma)$$
..where $(p,q)$ is the point of inflection. Point of inflections are a point just like the vertex of a quadratic function. It's where the concavity (similar concepts to gradient) changes.
it's very similar to the quadratic function -- just the exponent swapped. 
..The $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$ stands for the intercepts along the $x$-axis.

Let's look at this graph:

$y=2(x-1)^3+2$
Function is drawn using the inflection form.
As we can see, the cubic function has a point of inflection at $(1,2)$ marked in pink -- since the inflection point's equation states that $(p,q)$ is the point of inflection.

Moving onto the next form, we have the intercept form. Unline the inflection point form, the intercept form has a local maximum & minimum (local extrema). This is because when you expand the intercept form, you get a $x^2$. The $x^2$ makes it so that the cubic have a local max & min (more explanation on calculus using derivatives).

$y=(x-1)(x-2)(x-3)$

As you can see, the graph intercepts the $x$-axis at exactly $(1,0)$ $(2,0)$, and $(3,0)$.

## Trends: 
Trends are the ones you saw in stats (if you have done it). Is it an increasing trend? Or, a decreasing trend?

We read trends from left to right -- like you read a book. On the graphs above, both graphs have an increasing trend, because $a$, the scale factor is in the positives. 

If a function has a negative scale factor $(a)$, then you get a negative trend (try it on Desmos).
